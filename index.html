Forme Studio

Welcome & post-purchase email sequence for a premium skincare brand

FormatEmail Sequence
Emails4 (Welcome + Post-purchase)
FrameworkBAB + Retention
GoalActivate & retain first-time buyers

The Brief

Forme Studio is a premium skincare brand targeting women aged 25–40 who are serious about their routine but skeptical of overclaiming. The brand's tone is calm, considered, and quietly confident — no hype, no before/after shock tactics.

The brief: write a welcome sequence that converts new subscribers into first-time buyers, and a post-purchase sequence that turns first-time buyers into repeat customers. The copy needed to feel like a knowledgeable friend, not a brand talking at you.

Email Sequence

4-Part Email Sequence
01
Day 0 — Welcome
Subject: You made it here for a reason.

You didn't stumble onto Forme by accident.

Most people who find us have already been through the cycle — the trending serum that irritated their skin, the "clean" moisturiser that did nothing, the routine they built from YouTube recommendations that still didn't work.

We built Forme for exactly that point of frustration.

Every product we make starts with one question: what does this skin actually need, not what does it look good claiming to do? That means fewer ingredients, not more. Concentrations that are clinically meaningful. Formulas that don't fight each other.

Over the next few days, we'll share the thinking behind how we formulate — and help you figure out what your skin actually needs right now.

Nothing to buy yet. Just useful information.

→ Start with our skin diagnostic
— The Forme Team
02
Day 2 — Education
Subject: The ingredient your skin is probably missing.

If you've been dealing with dullness or uneven tone, there's a good chance niacinamide is the gap in your routine.

It's one of the most well-researched ingredients in skincare. It strengthens the skin barrier, reduces the appearance of pores, and helps regulate oil production — without irritating sensitive skin. Most people can use it twice a day.

The problem is concentration. Most products with niacinamide on the label use 1–2%. Studies show you need at least 5% for meaningful results. Our Clarifying Serum uses 10% — which sounds aggressive, but because we've buffered it with ceramides, almost nobody experiences irritation.

This is what we mean by formulation over marketing.

Day 5 — Social Proof + Offer
Subject: What three months actually looks like.

We don't show before/afters. Not because we don't have them — because we think they reduce skincare to a trick, and your skin deserves better than that.

What we will share: what customers tell us after 90 days.

If you've been thinking about trying us, now is a good time. Use WELCOME15 for 15% off your first order.

→ Shop with WELCOME15

04
Day 14 — Post-Purchase
Subject: Your order arrived. Here's what to do next.

Your Forme order should be with you by now.

A few things worth knowing before you start:

Week 1 is about consistency, not results. Your skin is adjusting. Don't layer everything at once — introduce one new product at a time so you can actually tell what's working.

If you're using the Clarifying Serum for the first time: start with evenings only for the first two weeks. Once your skin is used to it, you can use it morning and night.

Sunscreen in the morning, every day. Non-negotiable. Any brightening or exfoliating product becomes significantly less effective if you skip SPF.

We're here if you have questions — just reply to this email.

Copywriter's Note

Why this sequence works the way it does.

Forme's buyer isn't impulsive. She's done the research. She's been burned before. The biggest barrier isn't price — it's skepticism. So the sequence leads with education and proof of thinking, not product pushes.

Email 1 earns trust by demonstrating that the brand understands her frustration. Email 2 gives her something genuinely useful — ingredient knowledge she can apply anywhere, not just at Forme. By the time Email 3 makes an offer, she already trusts the brand's judgment. The post-purchase email reduces buyer's remorse and sets expectations — which is what drives repeat orders.

Framework used: BAB (Before/After/Bridge) across the sequence as a whole, with a retention-first structure in Email 4.

Lumé is a skincare brand whose hero product is a dark-spot correcting serum formulated specifically for melanin-rich skin. The audience has been underserved by mainstream skincare — most "brightening" products weren't developed with their skin in mind, and many have experienced uneven results or irritation from generic formulas.

The brief: write a landing page that converts skeptical buyers who've tried other products, and a 3-email sequence that supports them through an 8-week treatment cycle, reducing churn and driving reorders.

Landing Page

Landing Page Copy
Your skin doesn't need more products. It needs the right one.

Email Sequence

3-Part Treatment Sequence

Day 0 — Welcome

Before it arrives — a few things worth knowing.

Week 1 and 2 are quiet. That's normal. Tranexamic acid works inside the skin, not on top of it. You won't see dramatic change in the first two weeks, and that doesn't mean it's not working.

Copywriter's Note

Selling to a skeptical buyer with a history of disappointment.

The primary conversion barrier here isn't price — it's the "I've tried this before" objection. The landing page leads with that exact frustration in the first two lines, then immediately reframes it as a formula problem, not a personal one. That shift moves the reader from resigned to curious.

The email sequence mirrors a coaching relationship, not a sales funnel. Each email gives the buyer something useful at exactly the moment they need it: expectations before they start, troubleshooting at the halfway point, and a data-backed reorder nudge at the end. The goal is zero churn, not just one sale.

Framework: PAS on the landing page (Problem/Agitation/Solution), transitioning to retention-led nurture in the email sequence.

The Brief

Drape makes investment-grade wardrobe pieces for professional women who are tired of buying a lot and wearing little. The brand's philosophy is anti-fast-fashion: limited SKUs per season, a neutral palette designed for mix-and-match, and fabric quality that outlasts trends.

The brief: write a landing page that converts women who are already interested in intentional dressing but haven't yet committed to buying, and a welcome email sequence that educates new subscribers on the Drape approach before making an offer.

Landing Page

Landing Page Copy
Fewer clothes. Better mornings. Actually wearing everything you own.
Drape makes investment pieces for women who are tired of having a full wardrobe and nothing to wear.

Here's what most fashion brands won't say

You don't need more clothes. You need fewer clothes that work harder.

The reason you're standing in front of your wardrobe at 7am feeling stuck isn't because you don't have enough. It's because nothing quite goes with everything else. One good piece, but it needs the right shoes. Another that only works in one context. A drawer full of maybes.

Drape is built differently. Every piece we make is designed to work with at least five others in our range. Wear the linen trousers to a client meeting. Wear them to dinner. Wear them on a slow Saturday. The same item, three different days, zero redundancy.

  • Fabric that lasts, not fabric that photographs

If you're new to intentional dressing, starting small sounds counterintuitive. Here's why it works:

Copywriter's Note

Selling restraint in an industry built on excess.

The tension here is interesting: the brand's philosophy is "buy less," but the goal is to make a sale. The landing page resolves this by making the reader feel seen in her frustration before presenting Drape as a different kind of solution — not another thing to buy, but a system to replace the cycle she's stuck in.

The email sequence delays the sales pitch until Email 3 deliberately. By then, she's been given a concrete framework (the 3-piece starter) that makes the purchase feel like an intelligent decision, not an impulse. Email 3 uses a customer story rather than product copy, which works better for a skeptical, research-led buyer.

Framework: BAB (Before/After/Bridge) across the full sequence, with scarcity used honestly in Email 3 — limited batch is factual, not manufactured urgency.

Copywriter's Note

The activation problem: signed up isn't the same as converted.

Most SaaS churn happens during the trial, not after it. People sign up, get overwhelmed, and never come back. The onboarding sequence is designed to solve that by giving the user one clear task per email, timed to the moments when they're most likely to disengage.

Email 1 gives them a quick win — the welcome sequence setup — so they feel immediate value. Email 2 introduces the highest-differentiating feature (revenue tracking) when they're already inside the product. Email 3 removes the final conversion friction by making the upgrade feel like the natural next step, not a sales pitch.

Framework: AIDA on the landing page, activation-focused retention copy across the sequence. The trial-end email deliberately avoids countdown urgency language — the product either worked or it didn't, and the email acknowledges that honestly.
